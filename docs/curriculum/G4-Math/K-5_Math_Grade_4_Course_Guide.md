**Introduction**

Welcome to the Grade 4 Course Guide. This guide is designed to give Grade 4 teachers specific knowledge
about the structure of the instructional materials to help them effectively transition into the program.
Included in this guide is information about standards, assessment tools, the focus of each module, and ways
for families to support students at home. This guide also provides information about how to tailor the culture
of learning to the developmental needs of students, including a background on developmental advances
that affect how students learn in Grade 4 and practical strategies for troubleshooting misconceptions
collaboratively with students.


**Profile of a Grade 4 Math Learner**

Grades 2 through 4 cover a period when students‚Äô math abilities are rapidly changing, growing, and starting to
come together. Their motor skills, estimation abilities, abstract reasoning, self-regulation, and peer interaction
skills need to be coordinated for them to be successful with the instructional materials. Often, this means that
students may become confused or solve problems incorrectly because one or more of the requisite skills are
still developing. For example, students with developing motor skills may have difficulty with manipulating tiles
during activities that teach arrays.

Students at these grade levels are also at the very beginning of developing their abstract reasoning abilities.
For this reason, students can find it challenging to reason about inequalities with different units (e.g., that
10 cm is smaller than 5 m, despite 10 being a larger whole number than 5) or inequalities with different place
values (e.g., that 900 is less than 1,100, even though 9 is greater than 1). Students‚Äô concrete and rule-based
reasoning can also make it more challenging for them to grasp key principles in arithmetic (e.g., that 3 √ó 6 is
the same as 6 √ó 3).

In grades 2 through 4, many students are still developing into fluent readers, which might cause them to focus
on certain terms in a word problem and ignore the rest of the context (e.g., they look for the word _total_ and
then add the numbers in the problem). In addition, students‚Äô self-control is still developing. This might cause
students to rush to solve problems and write down the first answer they think of.

These emerging skills need to be coordinated to solve math problems correctly. As a result, teachers of
students in this age range play an important role in helping young math learners learn the math content
and in laying a foundation for number sense, arithmetic, problem solving, and more. This work will prepare
students for more complex and representational math later in the instructional materials. This means that
teachers need to look at the underlying reasons why a student might have gotten a problem wrong‚Äîwhether
it is an abstract reasoning skill, a literacy skill, or an impulsivity issue‚Äîand tailor instruction appropriately.

The modules in the _Bluebonnet Learning_ _K‚Äì5 Math_ instructional materials are designed to effectively help
teachers analyze students‚Äô work and provide the instructional support that best meets students‚Äô needs.
The activities support students‚Äô developmental needs by identifying student misconceptions and providing
guidance to address these misconceptions. This ensures that students can move away from an algorithmic,
rule-based version of math‚Äîwhich can often lead them astray as the concepts become more complex‚Äî
toward a more holistic competence with mathematical reasoning. For example, teachers are encouraged to
have students work in small groups on problems and explain their reasoning to one another. This approach
allows teachers and students to identify and correct misconceptions as they arise.

For students to benefit from the _Bluebonnet Learning_ _K‚Äì5 Math_ instructional materials approach, they need
to be provided with opportunities to engage in productive struggle. This means that students boldly and
confidently learn to confront mistakes, reframe them into learning opportunities, and then move forward,
deepening their mathematical reasoning throughout the year. One of the best ways to encourage students
to embrace productive struggle is to promote a classroom culture of learning. A culture of learning is one
in which students embrace the process of productive struggle. It is also a culture free from the common
idea that ‚Äúfast at math = good at math.‚Äù By creating a culture of learning and encouraging students to view
challenges as an expected and even desirable part of the learning process, teachers can help students avoid
a fear of math that could undermine the mathematical future of many students. Instead, students can start
enjoying and feeling confident in math even if their answers do not always come quickly.


**Culture of Learning in Grade 4**

As noted in the Program and Implementation Guide, the way to engage math learners and help them embrace
the productive struggle that‚Äôs needed to learn deeply is to create a culture of learning. [1] As shown in the figure
below, a culture of learning rests on a foundation of (1) high-quality instructional materials, (2) a teacher‚Äôs
intentions, and (3) the language that communicates the intent of teachers and materials.

A culture of learning can be created beginning on the first day of Module 1. A teacher can use language that
communicates their intent for all students to be supported as they are held to a high standard for learning. [2]

A culture of learning can also be maintained throughout the year since teachers will continue to support
math learners as they embrace challenge. For example, teachers can use language that clarifies their intent of
assigning the rigorous challenges students will face in the instructional materials.

and engagement because it doesn‚Äôt fit with their developmentally appropriate desire for autonomy and

**Collaboratively Troubleshooting Student Misconceptions**

It is common for students to make mistakes as they build their understanding of new or difficult concepts.
From a student perspective, mistakes can logically occur whenever students try to apply previous learning to
new problems that require a different approach. To help students correct their thinking and develop a better
understanding, teachers can identify the knowledge that students are building on when they make a mistake.

The _Bluebonnet Learning_ _K‚Äì5 Math_ instructional materials list common student mistakes for each module.
Teachers can use this information to troubleshoot mistakes collaboratively with students.

As noted in the Program and Implementation Guide, _collaborative troubleshooting_ is a routine to help
teachers address students‚Äô misconceptions. The three steps to collaborative troubleshooting are


(1) surface student thinking;

(2) validate what the student did right; and

(3) bridge to a better understanding.


In Step 1, it‚Äôs helpful to ask questions to better understand what the student was thinking when they made a
decision. For example, the teacher can ask, ‚ÄúCan you tell me more about your thinking on this problem?‚Äù
or ‚ÄúI noticed you wrote ____. What were some of your reasons for doing that?‚Äù

In Step 2, it‚Äôs helpful to point out the parts of the problem that the student understood or did correctly. That
way, the student maintains their successful thinking as they retry a problem. For example, teachers can say
‚ÄúI noticed you did ____ and wrote ____, and that‚Äôs great because that means you understood ____.‚Äù

Last, in Step 3, the teacher can build on the student‚Äôs assets and bridge to a better understanding. It can help
to ask guided questions that invite the student to build a stronger understanding. For example, the teacher
can ask the following questions:


‚¨õ
‚ÄúIf you keep ____ [correct thinking] and changed ____ [misconception] to ____ [alternative], then
what do you think might happen?‚Äù


‚¨õ
‚ÄúWhat would happen if you did ____ instead of ____?‚Äù


‚¨õ
‚ÄúIf ____ [correct thinking] is true, then how does that thinking affect ____ [misconception]?‚Äù

**Scenario** : A student makes an error on a multi-step word problem involving addition and subtraction, such as
with the following example.

In one year, a factory used 11,650 meters of cotton. They used 4,950 fewer meters of silk than cotton and
3,500 fewer meters of wool than silk. How many meters in all were used of the three fabrics?

The student believes that the question ‚ÄúHow many meters in all?‚Äù means the three numbers shown in the
problem should be added together without considering what each number represents. The teacher has a oneon-one discussion with the student.


**Step 1: Surface student thinking.**

T: Why did you decide to add 11,650, 4,950, and 3,500?
S: The question is asking ‚ÄúHow many meters in all?‚Äù So, I added the three numbers to find the total.


**Step 2: Validate what the student did right.**

T: Yes, the problem is asking you to find the total meters of fabric used. Let‚Äôs read the information given
in the problem about each number again.
S: A factory used 11,650 meters of cotton. They used 4,950 fewer meters of silk than cotton and 3,500
fewer meters of wool than silk.


**Step 3: Bridge to a better understanding.**

T: What do you notice about the numbers? Do we know the length of each type of fabric?
S: The length of cotton is 11,650 meters. The amounts of the other fabrics are based on the amounts of
cotton. We don‚Äôt know the length of silk or wool.
T: Let‚Äôs draw a strip diagram to represent the length of each type of fabric. First, read what the problem
says about cotton. How can you represent that in a strip diagram?
S: I can draw a strip for cotton and label it 11,650. (Draw.)
T: Read what the problem says about the amount of silk. How can you represent that in the strip
diagram? How does it compare to cotton?
S: I can draw a shorter strip for silk and label the difference between cotton and silk as 4,950. (Draw.)
T: Read what the problem says about the amount of wool. How can you represent that in the strip
diagram? How does it compare to the other parts of the diagram?
S: I can draw a strip for wool that is shorter than the silk strip and label the difference between silk and
wool as 3,500. (Draw.)
T: What does the strip diagram show you about how to find the amount of silk used?
S: I can subtract 4,950 from 11,650 to find the amount of silk used.
T: What does the strip diagram show you about how to find the amount of wool used?
S: I can subtract 3,500 from the amount of silk used.
S: (Subtracts and labels the strip diagram with the amounts of silk and wool used.)
T: How will you find how many meters of fabric used in all?
S: Now I know the amounts of silk and wool used. I can add the amounts of all three fabrics to find the
total.

monitor and track their own growth. It can also help students anticipate and process any thoughts they may
have about completing an assessment.

These discussions help the teacher promote a culture of productive struggle as students go into testing. These
discussions also support students‚Äô needs after testing and give students the opportunity to reflect on their

**Summary of Year**

Grade 4 mathematics is about (1) developing understanding and fluency with multi-digit multiplication,
and developing understanding of dividing to find quotients involving multi-digit dividends; (2) developing
an understanding of fraction equivalence, addition and subtraction of fractions with like denominators; and
(3) understanding that geometric figures can be analyzed and classified based on their properties, such as
having parallel sides, perpendicular line segments, particular angle measures, and symmetry.


**Rationale for Module Sequence in Grade 4**

In Grade 4, students extend their work with whole numbers. They begin with large numbers using familiar units
(hundreds and thousands) and develop their understanding of billions by building knowledge of the pattern of
_times ten_ in the base-ten system on the place value chart. In Grades 2 and 3, students focused on developing the
concept of composing and decomposing place value units within the addition and subtraction algorithms. Now,
in Grade 4, those compositions and decompositions are seen through the lens of multiplicative comparison (e.g.,
1 thousand is 10 times as much as 1 hundred). Students next apply their broadened understanding of patterns
on the place value chart to compare, round, add, and subtract. The addition and subtraction algorithms then
become efficient and useful applications of students‚Äô knowledge of and skill with composing and decomposing
higher value units. The module culminates with solving multi-step word problems involving addition and
subtraction modeled with strip diagrams that focus on numerical relationships.

The addition and subtraction algorithms continue to play a part in Module 2 as students relate place value
units to metric units. This module helps students draw similarities between the following:


1 ten = 10 ones
1 hundred = 10 tens


1 hundred = 100 ones
1 meter = 100 centimeters


1 thousand = 1,000 ones
1 kilometer = 1,000 meters
1 kilogram = 1,000 grams
1 liter = 1,000 milliliters

Students work with metric measurement as a context for using the addition and subtraction algorithms,
mental math, place value, and word problems. (Customary units are used as a context for fractions in
Modules 5 and 7.) Students extend their knowledge from Grade 3 to use input‚Äìoutput tables and numerical
expressions to represent number patterns and the relationship between the position of a number in a pattern
and the value of that number.

In Module 3, measurement of perimeter and area provide the concrete foundation behind the distributive
property in the multiplication algorithm. For example, 4 √ó (1 m 2 cm) can be modeled concretely using ribbon,
since students can see the 4 copies of 1 meter and the 4 copies of 2 centimeters. Likewise, 4 √ó (1 ten 2 ones)
= 4 tens 8 ones. Next, students use place value disks to develop efficient procedures and models that lead
to using the algorithms for multiplying by one-digit whole numbers. They understand and explain why the
procedures work, and they make connections between the area model and work on the place value chart.
Building on their knowledge of division types ( _group size unknown_ and _number of groups unknown_ ), students
represent division with arrays and the area model, before practicing with place value disks and then the
standard division algorithm. Interpreting remainders, both in word problems and long division, is a focus of
this work. Finally, two-digit by two-digit multiplication is modeled using the area model, extending students‚Äô
earlier experiences with measurement and the distributive property. Throughout the module, students also
solve word problems where they select and accurately apply appropriate methods to estimate, mentally
calculate, or use written strategies to compute products and quotients.

Module 4 focuses on solving unknown angle measure problems using equations with letters standing for unknowns
and on constructing, analyzing, classifying two-dimensional figures, and recognizing and drawing lines of symmetry.
From their work in earlier grades, students have already used letters and equations to solve word problems. They
continue to do so in Grade 4, and now they also learn to solve unknown angle problems, work that challenges
students to build and solve equations to find unknown angle measures. First, students learn the definition of _degree_
and learn how to measure angles in degrees using a circular protractor. From the definition of _degree_ and the fact
that angle measures are additive, the following rudimentary facts about angles naturally follow:


1. The sum of angle measurements around a point is 360 degrees.
2. The sum of angle measurements on a line is 180 degrees.


Hence, from 1 and 2, students see that vertical angles have equal measure. Armed only with these facts,
students are able to generate and solve equations as in the following problem:
